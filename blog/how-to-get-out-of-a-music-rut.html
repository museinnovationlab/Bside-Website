How to get out of a music rut

July 8, 2026 · 6 min read

You have access to roughly every recorded song in human history, and you've played the same twelve albums since March. This is the strange, specific poverty of abundance — and it's not your fault.

A rut isn't a shortage of music

Worth naming plainly: you are not in a rut because there's nothing good out there. Something like a hundred thousand tracks are uploaded to streaming services every day. The supply is not the problem.

A rut is a decision problem. When you open a music app, you're not asked "what do you want to hear?" — you're asked to choose from infinity, immediately, usually while doing something else. Infinity is a terrible menu. So you do the rational thing and pick the sure bet, again. Do that for four months and you've built a rut out of nothing but a series of individually sensible choices.

Why they happen

Three forces, usually working together:

Comfort under load. When life is busy or stressful, novelty costs energy you don't have. Familiar music is a known quantity — no risk of a bad forty minutes. Ruts often aren't taste problems; they're bandwidth problems.

Choice paralysis. Faced with everything, most people default to something. The bigger the catalogue, the stronger this pull. Endless options don't create adventure; they create defaults.

Recommendation loops. Personalized feeds learn from what you play. Play the safe thing, get more safe things, play those. The system narrows around you, politely, over months.

Seven ways out

1. Diagnose it first: are you bored, or just busy?

If your life is currently loud — new job, new baby, hard season — you may not need new music. You may need twenty undistracted minutes with music you already love. Not every rut needs solving; some need permission.

2. Shrink the choice, don't widen it

The instinct is "I need more options." The opposite works better. Narrow it to one decision: one new record, this week. Constraint is what makes a choice possible. Infinity is what got you here.

3. Change the unit

Stop shuffling. Put on one album, start to finish, without your phone. Shuffling is browsing; an album is reading. You've probably not been bored by music so much as skimming it.

4. Go backwards to go forwards

Take a record that mattered to you at seventeen and find out what it was listening to. Every album has parents. Following influence backwards is more reliable than any recommendation engine, because the connections were made by the artists themselves.

5. Follow the credits

Find the producer of an album you love and see what else they made. Same with the label, the engineer, the session players. A great producer is a curator with a sound — and their catalogue is a hand-built playlist nobody advertises.

6. Borrow better ears

Find one critic, newsletter, or aggressively opinionated friend whose taste overlaps yours by about 70%. Not 100% — that's a mirror, and mirrors are how you got stuck. The 30% you disagree with is the whole point.

7. Lower the stakes

You don't have to overhaul your identity. You have to listen to one unfamiliar record. If it's bad, you've lost forty minutes. That's the entire downside, and it's much smaller than the thing you've been avoiding.
